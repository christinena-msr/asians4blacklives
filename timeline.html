Timeline of Black History


1619

First enslaved Africans arrive in America

The first enslaved Africans, kidnapped by the Portuguese from modern-day Angola, were brought to Jamestown, Virginia

1787

The Three-Fifths Compromise

The Three-Fifths Compromise, decided at the Constitutional Convention, stated that three-fifths of the total slave population would be counted for determining taxes and representation in the House of Representatives.

1820

Missouri Compromise

This compromise admitted Maine as a free state and Missouri as a slave state, keeping a North-South balance. The government used the 36°30' line to divide free from slave states.



1852

Uncle Tom's Cabin

Harriet Beecher Stowe's novel Uncle Tom's Cabin was released in March of 1852, galvanizing Northern opinions against slavery and laying the groundwork for the Civil War.

1850

Compromise of 1850

This compromise decided slave vs. free states in territory gained from the Mexican-American War.

July 5, 1852

I say it with a sad sense of the disparity between us. I am not included within the pale of this glorious anniversary! Your high independence only reveals the immeasurable distance between us. The blessings in which you, this day, rejoice, are not enjoyed in common. — The rich inheritance of justice, liberty, prosperity and independence, bequeathed by your fathers, is shared by you, not by me. The sunlight that brought life and healing to you, has brought stripes and death to me. This Fourth [of] July is yours, not mine. You may rejoice, I must mourn.


—Frederick Douglass, What to the Slave is the Fourth of July?

1854

Kansas-Nebraska Act

This Act repealed the Missouri Compromise, allowing territories above the 36°30' line popular sovereignty. This meant that the settlers in the territory could decide whether slavery would be allowed.



1859

John Brown

John Brown led armed abolitionists to storm Harper's Ferry, Virginia. They were soundly defeated by Robert E. Lee. Brown was subsequently convicted of treason.




1857

Scott vs. Sanders

Dred Scott, a slave, lived with his master on both sides of the slave vs. free line in Missouri (pre-Kansas-Nebraska Act), but lived mainly on the free side. He sued for his freedom after his master died, and lived both as a free man and as a slave until the Supreme Court decision. They decided that Scott was a slave - not a citizen - and therefore could not be afforded the same privileges as American citizens.

1861 - 1865

Civil War

Southern states seceded to create the Confederacy. Abraham Lincoln proposed paid compensation for emancipated slaves and colonization of slaves to Latin America. These deliberations eventually led to the Emancipation Proclamation.

1863

Emancipation Proclamation

The Emancipation Proclamation declared that slaves in states in open rebellion to the Union were freed. At the time, this did not mean much as the Confederacy did not recognize the Union government or Lincoln as their president.

1865

Abolition of Slavery

The thirteenth amendment was passed to abolish slavery, except as punishment for crime.


Founding of Ku Klux Klan

1865-1968

Post-Civil War Era


Reconstruction Era (1865-1877)

Presidential Reconstruction (1865-1867)

President Andrew Johnson pardoned the South, except for Confederate leaders and wealthy planters. Johnson allowed a Southern government to form, which started passing "Black codes," outlining how to continue using freed Black slaves for labor. The Civil Rights Act of 1866 declared that all people born in the United States were U.S. citizens and had certain inalienable rights. The fourteenth amendment, passed in 1868, gave citizenship to all who were born or naturalized in the United States, giving freed Black people citizenship.


Radical/Congressional Reconstruction (1867-1877)

The South divided into five military districts, which were controlled by the North. The fifteenth amendment passed in 1870, which allowed citizens the right to vote, regardless of race. Both the North and the South saw an increase in Black representation in office.


Redemption

Redemption saw increased anxiety in the South about economics and states' rights. After being effectively quashed in 1868, the KKK rose again as Northern occupation ended in the South. There was increased destruction of Black property and threatening of Black lives at voting booths. Voter suppression policies arose, included gerrymandering, poll taxes, grandfather clauses, and literacy tests. Lynching rates skyrocketed, many lynchings taking place under the guise of protecting white womanhood.


Niagara Movement (1905)

W.E.B. DuBois, other Black supporters, and white abolitionists gathered on the Canadian side of Niagara Falls to create a list of demands for ending segregation, discrimination based on race, and assurance of economic equality and opportunity.


Formation of NAACP (1909)

Formed by Black leaders including W.E.B. DuBois and Ida B. Wells, the main aims of the NAACP were to promote equality of rights, eradicate race prejudice, and increase opportunities for Black lives.


Great Migration

Migration of Black people from South to North to escape voter suppression and sharecropping in favor of factory jobs. These "scab laborers" were vilified by white union workers.

1870s

Start of Jim Crow laws

1896

Plessy v. Ferguson

Homer Plessy, who was 7/8 white and 1/8 Black, was arrested for boarding a "whites only" train car, instead of sitting in one of the "equal, but separate" cars for Black people. The Supreme Court upheld the constitutionality of segregation laws as long as segregated facilities were equal in quality. This decision led to the phrase "separate but equal."

1898

Wilmington Race Massacre

Coup d'etat of local government with Black Republican polticians by massacreing a prosperous Black neighborhood.

1915

Release of "The Birth of a Nation"

Civil Rights Movement


Brown v. Board of Education

Desegregation of schools



Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955-1956)

After the injustice against Rosa Parks in attempt to end segregated buses, approximately 40,000 Black citizens in Montgomery, Alabama boycott buses for 381 days.



Formation of Student Non-Violent Coordinating Committee (1960)

Principal channel of student commitment for the Civil Rights Movement



Formation of Black Panther Party (1966)

Bobby Seale & Huey Newton in Oakland, California

1955

Emmett Till murdered

Emmett Till, 14, was accused of flirting with a white woman at a store he visited in Money, Mississippi. After hearing of the incident, the woman's husband and his half-brother lynched Till and threw him in the Tallahatchie River. His body was found three days later. The men were found not guilty of the murder at trial, but openly confessed one year later.

1957

Little Rock Nine

Nine Black children enrolled at the all-white Central High School in Little Rock, Arkansas. On their first day of classes, the governor ordered the National Guard to block their entry. The National Guard was removed by federal order, and the police escorted the nine students inside. Their names were Minnijean Brown, Elizabeth Eckford, Ernest Green, Thelma Mothershed, Melba Patillo, Gloria Ray, Terrence Roberts, Jefferson Thomas and Carlotta Walls.

1960

Ruby Bridges attends William Frantz Elementary School

Ruby Bridges becomes the first Black child to attend the all-white school in New Orleans, Louisiana. She was one of six Black children who passed an entrance exam to allow them to attend the school. Two of the children decided to stay at their own school, while the other three went to McDonogh No. 19. They were Leona Tate, Tessie Prevost, and Gail Etienne, nicknamed the McDonogh Three.

1971

Nixon Declares War on Drugs

One of the many things white people in power did to criminalize and take advantage of the 13th Amendment loophole. The beginning of mass incarceration of minorities (especially Black and Latino men).

1986

Reagan Administration: "Just Say No"

Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1986

After Nixon, Reagan continued to utilize the criminalization of drugs in order to target and imprison minorities. Black people are dealt much harsher sentences for possession of almost the equivalent drug as white people. Cocaine becomes a "suburb" drug, while crack cocaine flooded urban areas with high Black population.

1989

Central Park 5

Five boys are sentenced to life in prison after being convicted of the rape and assault of a woman in Central Park. None of these boys had any conection to the crime.

1992

LA Riots

Rodney King was brutally beat by police officers during his DUI arrest. The jury acquitted 4 officers from "usage of excess force". These officers faced no punishment for their actions. Riots erupted in mostly Koreatown partially due to the racial tensions between Koreans and Black people.

1994

Violent Crime Control and Law Enforcement Act

This new bill provided for 100,000 more police officers to "fight" crime. $9.7 billion in prison funding, and $6.1 billion in prevention programs.


Three Stikes Law

This new law mandated life sentence in prison for 3 convictions. Many states have enacted similar laws at the state level.

2008

Obama Wins the Presidential Election

Barack Obama becomes the first Black man to be elected President of the United States of America.
